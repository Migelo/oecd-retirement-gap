In every OECD country,
women outlast men in retirement.

Two forces stack on top of each other. Women simply live longer — and in a shrinking handful of countries they are still allowed to stop working earlier. The result is a stretch of years, after the last paycheck and before life expectancy runs out, that is consistently longer for women than for men.

What drives it

Five things the spans reveal

  1. It is now mostly about longevity, not policy

    Most of the OECD has equalized statutory retirement ages. So the typical gap of two to four years is driven almost entirely by women outliving men. The countries with the smallest gaps — Iceland, New Zealand, Norway, the United States — all pair a unisex age with a modest longevity difference.

  2. The remaining gendered ages produce the widest gaps

    A few countries still let women retire earlier — Poland (65 / 60), Colombia (62 / 57), Costa Rica, and Chile's funded pillar — with Austria, Switzerland and Israel phasing the gap out. A lower age compounds with longer life. Poland is the extreme: a woman retiring at 60 can expect about 25 years of retirement against roughly 16 for a man — an 8.6-year gap.

  3. The Baltics show the opposite story

    Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania have fully unisex ages of 65, yet still post gaps near five years. That is pure mortality: Baltic men have some of the OECD's lowest life expectancy at 65 — around 14.5 remaining years against 19.5 for women. Equal ages, deeply unequal lifespans, and one of the shortest male retirements in the developed world.

  4. Statutory is not the same as actual

    These spans use the statutory normal age. Real labour-market exit is often earlier (France, Luxembourg, Austria) or later (Korea, where women work to about 67). Hungary's "Women 40" rule and Chile's dual scheme mean realized female retirement runs even longer than the bars suggest.

  5. Ages are rising — the longevity gap is not closing

    Retirement ages are converging on 67, and a growing group now auto-indexes the age to life expectancy: Denmark, Finland, Italy, Portugal, Greece, the Netherlands, Estonia, Sweden and Norway. That compresses the retirement window over time and erases the policy gap — but it leaves the longevity-driven gap between the sexes largely intact.

Method & caveats

How to read this honestly

The core metric. "Expected years in retirement" is remaining life expectancy at the normal retirement age. It folds the retirement-age gap and the lifespan gap into one number. Where the age is exactly 65 it equals life expectancy at 65; where it differs, the value is interpolated from life expectancy at 65 using standard life-table decrements (about 0.8–0.9 years per year of age). Treat figures as accurate to roughly half a year — don't over-read a single decimal.

Statutory, not effective. Ages are the statutory normal retirement age for a full-career worker, current or legislated for 2025–2026. The OECD's official "expected years in retirement" indicator is measured from the effective labour-market exit age instead, which is usually earlier — so its numbers run larger, especially in France, Luxembourg and Austria.

Data years. Life expectancy is mostly 2022–2023 period life tables (UN World Population Prospects, cross-checked against OECD Pensions at a Glance, Eurostat, the World Bank and national statistical offices). Period — not cohort — life expectancy is used throughout. Türkiye is not comparable on the statutory measure and is best read via its effective-exit figure (men ≈18, women ≈24).

Sources. OECD Pensions at a Glance 2023 & 2025; Finnish Centre for Pensions retirement-age comparison; UN World Population Prospects 2022/2023; Eurostat mortality statistics; World Bank WDI; and national statistical offices (ABS, StatCan, ONS, CDC/NCHS, CBS Israel, Stats NZ and others).
